# Avrah Illustration Style Guide

> This guide is for the illustrator, designer, or team creating Avrah's illustration system. It gives you the creative direction, practical constraints, and enough freedom to make something better than what's described here.

---

## The Job

Avrah builds connected hardware and software — embedded systems, IoT devices, cloud platforms, dashboards. The work is deeply technical. The clients are serious. And the illustration system needs to make all of that feel **approachable, warm, and human** without undermining the credibility of the engineering.

Most IoT and embedded studios look like they're selling to other engineers. Avrah wants to look like it's *made by* engineers who remember that real people use the things they build.

The illustration system carries more of Avrah's personality than any other brand element. The logo and typography are restrained. The colors are controlled. The illustrations are where the warmth lives.

---

## Style Principles

### 1. Cute and Capable

This is the core tension. The illustrations should make someone smile and then realize "oh, these people clearly know what they're doing." A cute device with accurate pin layouts. A friendly cloud character with proper TLS handshake symbols. The charm comes from the contrast between the playful surface and the technical accuracy underneath.

**Reference feel:** Studio Ghibli's mechanical designs — technically detailed machines rendered with warmth and personality.

### 2. Small and Contained

Every illustration is a **self-contained little world**. Not a sprawling scene — a compact vignette with clear boundaries. Think terrarium, not landscape. Each one tells a complete story within its frame.

Typical composition: a small "island" or "platform" with elements arranged on or around it. Objects have gentle shadows that anchor them to their surface. The scene floats in space or sits on a subtle ground plane.

### 3. Soft Geometry

Shapes are geometric but never sharp. Rounded corners on everything. Gentle curves where a hard edge would do. Circuit boards have rounded traces. Device enclosures have pillow-soft edges. Antennas have slightly bulbous tips.

**Not flat design.** There's subtle depth — gentle gradients, soft shadows, a hint of dimension. Not full 3D rendering. Think 2.5D or isometric-adjacent. Enough depth to feel tangible, not enough to feel rendered.

### 4. Technical Accuracy Matters

The details should be *right*. If there's a circuit board, the traces should look like real traces — not random squiggles. If there's a BLE symbol, it should be the actual Bluetooth logo. If there's a sensor, it should resemble a real sensor form factor.

Engineers will look at these illustrations. They should see things they recognize and think "these people get it."

Accuracy isn't about photorealism — it's about showing real things with personality.

### 5. Nothing Is Static

Every scene should have a sense of **gentle motion or activity**. Data flowing. Signals radiating. LEDs blinking. Sparks appearing. Even in a still illustration, the viewer should feel like they caught the scene mid-action. Little motion lines, floating particles, radiating waves — subtle signs of life.

## Color in Illustrations

### Palette

Illustrations use the **brand gold** as the dominant warm accent, plus the **illustration accent palette**:

| Color | Hex | Use For |
|-------|-----|---------|
| **Avrah Gold** | `#c9a55a` | Dominant accent. Glows, signals, highlights, sparks, active LEDs. |
| **Amber** | `#d4865a` | Physical hardware, warm components, enclosures. |
| **Sage** | `#7a9e7e` | PCBs, circuit boards, health/status indicators. |
| **Copper** | `#b87333` | Traces, metallic elements, solder, connectors. |
| **Dusk** | `#8b7ec8` | Wireless signals, data streams, Bluetooth/RF. |
| **Sky** | `#6b9ec4` | Cloud elements, connectivity, infrastructure. |
| **Rose** | `#c47878` | Alerts, error states, warmth in human elements. |

### Color Rules

- **3-4 accent colors per illustration maximum.** Not all six. Choose colors based on the story each illustration tells.
- **Gold is always present** as the primary accent. It's the thread that ties the illustration system to the broader brand.
- **Backgrounds:** Obsidian (#0a0a0a) for dark contexts. Bone (#f0ebe0) for light contexts. Never pure white or pure black.
- **Muted, never saturated.** The accent colors above are already correct saturation. Don't crank them up. The warmth comes from the relationships between colors, not their intensity.
- **Gradients are allowed** but keep them subtle. Gold-to-amber for warmth. Dusk-to-sky for data/signal flows. Always within the palette.

## The Spark in Illustrations

The **spark motif** (four-pointed star, see `assets/spark.svg`) appears in illustrations wherever something is being **created, connected, or transformed**. It's the visual thread of *avra k'davra*.

**Where it appears:**
- At the point where a device sends its first signal
- Where a solder iron touches a pad
- At the apex of the Avrah mark within scenes
- When data arrives at a cloud endpoint
- In the moment a prototype becomes a finished product
- As ambient particles in active scenes

**How it appears:**
- Small (4-8px in context)
- Gold, often with a subtle glow (gold at 20% opacity behind it)
- Never more than 3-5 in a single illustration
- Varies in size — one larger, a couple smaller — for natural scatter

## Concept Directions

These are the illustration territories to develop. Not all need to ship at once. Start with whichever resonates, and expand from there.

### 1. Tiny Worlds

Small, self-contained scene for each domain Avrah works in. Each is a vignette — a floating island, a compact room, a miniature landscape — that tells a specific story about the work.

**Scenes to develop:**

| Scene | Domain | What it shows |
|-------|--------|---------------|
| The Neighborhood | Smart infrastructure | A cute block with tiny parking sensors poking from the ground, street lights with connectivity icons, a small data stream flowing to a dashboard screen on a pole. |
| The Hospital Room | Healthcare IoT | A cozy room with a friendly gateway device on the nightstand, soft BLE waves floating to a bedside monitor, a warm glow from a status LED. A tiny heart rate waveform on the screen. |
| The Rail Line | Transit / Predictive maintenance | A small train rolling along a track segment with sensor nodes. Data ripples radiating from the rails. A miniature dashboard floating above showing condition graphs. |
| The Workshop | Product development | A desk scene with a breadboard, a laptop showing code, scattered components, a coffee mug, and a finished device at the end of the desk. The spark motif bridges the mess to the finished product. |
| The Cloud | Infrastructure | A whimsical data center scene. Tiny servers with blinking LEDs, data streams flowing upward into a soft cloud shape, encryption locks floating at the boundary. |

**Composition guidelines:**
- Each scene sits on a floating platform or within a contained boundary
- Consistent isometric-ish perspective across scenes (not strict isometric — looser, more illustrated)
- Scene diameter: roughly equivalent in visual weight
- Each scene has one clear focal point and 2-3 supporting details

### 2. The Transformation Series

Three illustrations showing the same product at three stages — concept, prototype, production. Maps directly to the hero tagline: "concept to prototype, prototype to production, production to scale."

**Stage 1: Concept**
A napkin sketch with coffee stain rings. Hand-drawn wireframes. Arrows and annotations. Messy, human, full of potential. The spark motif appears at the tip of the pen.

**Stage 2: Prototype**
A breadboard with wires everywhere. Loose components. A laptop with a terminal open. Jumper cables. Beautiful chaos. An LED glowing gold — the first sign of life. A spark at the LED.

**Stage 3: Production**
A clean, finished device in someone's hand. Smooth enclosure. A small status LED glowing. Behind it, a subtle ghost outline of the breadboard fading away. A spark at the transition point.

**Usage:** These three can appear as a horizontal triptych on the process section, or as individual spot illustrations.

### 3. Hands Making Things

Illustrated hands as the consistent element across a set of spot illustrations. No faces, no full characters — just hands in action.

| Scene | Hands doing what |
|-------|-----------------|
| Sketching | Pen on paper, drawing a circuit diagram |
| Soldering | Iron touching a board, spark at the contact point |
| Coding | Fingers on keyboard, screen reflection in glasses (implied) |
| Assembling | Fitting a PCB into an enclosure |
| Holding | Presenting a finished device, palm up |
| Shipping | Placing a device into a box with care |

**Style notes:**
- Hands are drawn with personality — slightly rounded, not anatomically rigid
- Diverse skin tones across the set
- Each hand illustration works as a standalone spot illustration
- Consistent scale and framing

### 4. The Invisible Layer

Cross-section illustrations that reveal the hidden engineering inside seemingly simple objects.

A cute device shown from the outside on one side, cut away to reveal:
- Tiny labeled components (MCU, antenna, battery)
- Signal paths visualized as flowing lines
- Encryption represented as small lock icons
- Data flowing through layers (device → BLE → gateway → cloud → dashboard)

**This concept is core to Avrah's story:** we build everything you can't see. Making the invisible visible, and making it friendly.

### 5. The 404 Scene (Reference Implementation)

See `404.html` for a working implementation. A small device on a floating circuit board island, searching for a lost signal. Stars and sparks in the background. Broken trace on the board.

This is the **reference illustration** for the brand — if an illustrator can look at the SVG 404 scene and translate it into their style with more warmth, detail, and personality, the system is working.

## Commissioning Guidelines

### What to provide illustrators

1. This guide (the creative direction)
2. `BRAND_GUIDELINES.md` (color values, overall brand context)
3. The SVG assets (spark motif, mark) as reference
4. The `404.html` as a style reference
5. The original `BRAND_BRIEF.md` (the full story)

### What to ask for

- **Initial delivery:** 3-5 concept sketches exploring style before committing to full illustrations
- **First illustration:** The 404 scene or one Tiny World — something self-contained that tests the style
- **Expansion:** Once the style is approved, the remaining scenes can be developed in batches
- **Format:** SVG preferred for web, with PNG exports at 2x for fallback. Each illustration should work at 400-600px width on screen.
- **Layered files:** Request editable source files (Illustrator, Figma, or Procreate) so the system can be extended later

### What to evaluate

When reviewing illustration work, ask:

1. **Would an engineer trust this company?** The illustration should feel smart, not naive.
2. **Does it make you smile?** Even a little. If it's purely informational, something's missing.
3. **Is the technical detail right?** Real components, real signals, real concepts — not generic "tech" imagery.
4. **Does it feel like Avrah?** Warm gold. Restrained palette. Soft geometry. The spark motif. Quiet confidence.
5. **Does it work at the intended size?** Details should reward close looking but the scene should read clearly at card-illustration scale (~400px).

## What This System Is Not

- **Not mascot-based.** There is no Avrah character. The spark motif is the closest thing, and it's a shape, not a character.
- **Not icon-based.** These are illustrations with scenes, depth, and story — not flat icons or generic line art.
- **Not decorative.** Every illustration communicates something specific about Avrah's work. If it's just filling space, it doesn't belong.
- **Not precious.** The system should be easy to extend. An illustrator should be able to create a new scene that feels like it belongs without needing approval on every detail.
